Test for usability

User research

When usability testing with people with disabilities there are a few factors to consider on top of the standard process:

  • Make sure the testing material (e.g. website) is technically accessible first, especially before putting it in front of people using screenreaders or voice-input.
  • When usability testing with people who use assistive technologies, it is important to have some understanding of how their technology works in order to understand when you have hit a dead end. Run through the tasks with a screenreader so you are aware of where there might be blockers.
  • Encourage people to bring their own laptop/devices, and plan any recording around that. It takes less time to adapt the recording than for someone to adapt to a device they haven't used before.
  • Send any forms (such as data protection) in an accessible word document well before the session. Participants should not have to read a paper copy in order to take part. but it is ok to sign the paper copy so long as it is identical to the version they were sent.
  • Is the building accessible? Can someone with a wheel chair get to the room?
  • Plan to meet people at the front door of the building and escort them to the room.
  • Allow for an extra 15 minutes setup time and potentially breaks during the session, so leave buffer around the session times.

As a starting point it can help to pair up with another researcher who has done accessibility testing, or plan a session with an external provider to assist you getting experience in the type of testing.
